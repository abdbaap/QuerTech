Key Concepts Explained

Ray Tracing vs. Path Tracing

Ray Tracing simulates light rays for realistic lighting, shadows, and reflections. Path Tracing is a more advanced technique that follows entire light paths, offering greater accuracy but at a much higher computational cost. While ray tracing might render light accurately for direct reflections, path tracing can capture more complex inter-reflections and light scattering, leading to more physically accurate global illumination. The performance impact of path tracing is significantly greater, often resulting in a substantial drop in frames per second compared to standard rasterization or even ray tracing alone.

DLSS vs. FSR

Both DLSS (Deep Learning Super Sampling) by NVIDIA and FSR (FidelityFX Super Resolution) by AMD are upscaling technologies designed to boost frame rates. DLSS uses AI and dedicated Tensor Cores on NVIDIA GPUs for superior image reconstruction, often resulting in a sharper image. FSR is open-source and works on a broader range of GPUs (including NVIDIA and Intel), making it more accessible, though its image quality can sometimes be slightly less refined than DLSS. For 4K gaming, where rendering millions of pixels per frame is demanding, these technologies are invaluable for achieving playable frame rates without a drastic visual sacrifice.

Frame Generation & Latency

Frame Generation techniques, like NVIDIA's DLSS Frame Generation or AMD's Fluid Motion Frames, insert generated frames between traditionally rendered ones. This dramatically increases the perceived FPS, making gameplay feel incredibly smooth. However, this process can introduce additional latency, making it less suitable for highly competitive, fast-paced games where split-second reaction times are critical. It's often better suited for single-player or less latency-sensitive titles.
